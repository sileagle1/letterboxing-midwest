RE: [LetterboxingMidwest] Re: State Permits to place boxes? (Iowa)

From: Debbie Kotlarek (kotlarek@wi.rr.com) | Date: 2009-08-25 09:08:34 UTC-05:00

When we plant a box, we DO take a piece of paper of notebook along to jot down pertinent information.  We usually note the time we start and then anything that we might use as a landmark and general comments on the terrain.  We won’t necessarily use all the info, but by having lots of things it lets us pick & choose what we’ll use.  We then note what time we finish.  We finalize clues at home, perhaps making them into a coded format, a story, or whatever.  However we almost always include a note about the terrain and the approximate distance or time to complete.  This helps people make a more informed decision as to whether it is a hike they would like to do, and if they have enough time to do it.

 

We do plenty of letterboxing in the winter and leave all our boxes out regardless of what state they are in.  The boxes generally survive quite well – the cold doesn’t bother them.  However it is important to use a very good container that is waterproof.  Lock ‘n Locks are best, but some of the Dollar store knockoffs may also work fine.

 

We don’t participate in the other rubber stamping activities you mentioned, so can’t comment on them.

 

Wisconsin Hiker

Re: [LetterboxingMidwest] Re: State Permits to place boxes? (Iowa)

From: Atom118 (Aatom118@gmail.com) | Date: 2009-08-25 09:38:34 UTC-05:00
For state permits, we try to be aware of the local regulations about
planting boxes. We don't plant them in state parks or national parks
that have posted regulations. This also covers certain counties within
the states. If we are going out of town to plant a box, we try to ask
the locals if they know of any posted regulations. If nothing is
posted, then we take the fall back position of DADT like everyone else.
For planting in unfamiliar parks, we usually just find a park that, on
paper, looks like it might contain an appropriate surrounding for a
box. We shy away from parks that don't have lots of forested areas or
that seem to have busy locations. With that in mind, we'll have a box,
in hand, ready to go. We then go off in a direction that seems to offer
a hike of an appropriate length. As we go along, we usually write the
clue in our head and we jot down notes. When we go home, we write the
clues that day (or at the latest the next) while the clues are still
fresh in our minds. Depending on the length of the hike and available
landmarks, this process can be quick or can take a decent while.

As for boxes, they are usually very sturdy. However, you need to be
sure that they are sturdy when you place them. Make sure they don't
leak when squeezed, make sure they can likely withstand a moderate
amount of force, and be sure they aren't easily opened. If a box (We
like the Lock and Locks) can pass those tests, it will likely not allow
moisture inside, tolerate a branch falling on it, and survive curious
critters.

And for winter, its a GREAT time to plant boxes. If your box is well
hidden in the winter, it will be VERY WELL hidden in the spring after
all the plants bloom. Be sure to avoid areas (just like when you plant
in spring) that will likely be covered with lakes of snow melt. Winter
means you don't have to worry as much about ichy/pointy plants,
stinging/biting animals, or being spotted by muggles who tend to only
come out to enjoy the summer's sun. Just be sure to use landmarks for
clues that will be present in the spring/summer, and don't go searching
for boxes that reference things on the ground that might be covered in
snow. On the other hand, it is a good time time plan for spring and get
some carving done...either for traditional boxes, LTCs, or postals
(depending on what you like).
